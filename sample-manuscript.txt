Title: Efficacy of Mindfulness-Based Interventions in Reducing Burnout Among Emergency Medicine Physicians: A Systematic Review and Meta-Analysis

ABSTRACT

Background: Physician burnout has reached epidemic proportions globally, with emergency medicine (EM) physicians disproportionately affected due to the high-acuity, high-volume nature of their work environment. Mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs) have emerged as a promising strategy for reducing occupational stress and burnout; however, evidence specific to emergency medicine remains fragmented and inconclusive.

Objective: This systematic review and meta-analysis aims to evaluate the efficacy of mindfulness-based interventions in reducing burnout, improving psychological well-being, and enhancing professional quality of life among emergency medicine physicians.

Methods: A comprehensive search of PubMed, MEDLINE, EMBASE, PsycINFO, and the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials was conducted from inception to December 2024. Randomized controlled trials (RCTs) and quasi-experimental studies examining MBIs in emergency medicine physicians were included. The primary outcome was burnout as measured by validated instruments including the Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) and the Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI). Secondary outcomes included emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, personal accomplishment, and overall well-being. Risk of bias was assessed using the Cochrane Risk of Bias Tool 2.0. Meta-analyses were performed using random-effects models.

Results: Fourteen studies involving 1,847 emergency medicine physicians met inclusion criteria. MBIs were associated with statistically significant reductions in overall burnout scores (standardized mean difference [SMD] = -0.68, 95% CI: -0.89 to -0.47, p < 0.001) and emotional exhaustion (SMD = -0.72, 95% CI: -0.94 to -0.50, p < 0.001). Depersonalization scores also showed meaningful improvement (SMD = -0.54, 95% CI: -0.76 to -0.32, p < 0.001). Personal accomplishment scores increased significantly across intervention groups (SMD = 0.48, 95% CI: 0.27 to 0.69, p < 0.001). Heterogeneity was moderate (I² = 52%), and subgroup analyses revealed that structured eight-week programs demonstrated superior outcomes compared to brief interventions (p = 0.03).

Conclusions: Mindfulness-based interventions represent an evidence-based strategy for mitigating burnout among emergency medicine physicians. Eight-week structured programs demonstrate the most robust outcomes. Institutional adoption of these interventions may yield meaningful improvements in physician well-being, patient safety, and healthcare system sustainability.

Keywords: physician burnout, mindfulness, emergency medicine, systematic review, meta-analysis, occupational stress, psychological well-being

1. INTRODUCTION

Physician burnout is a multidimensional syndrome characterized by emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, and a diminished sense of personal accomplishment, arising as a response to chronic occupational stress (Maslach & Leiter, 2016). The global prevalence of burnout among physicians has been estimated at 67%, with emergency medicine consistently ranking among the highest-risk specialties (Rotenstein et al., 2018; West et al., 2018).

Emergency physicians operate within environments defined by clinical unpredictability, time pressure, high-acuity patient presentations, moral distress, and systemic inefficiencies including overcrowding and boarding. These structural stressors, compounded by administrative burden and inadequate institutional support, create fertile conditions for the progression from occupational stress to full syndromal burnout (Shanafelt et al., 2015).

The consequences of burnout extend beyond individual physician suffering. Burnout is independently associated with increased medical errors, diminished patient satisfaction, reduced quality of care, and elevated rates of physician attrition from clinical practice (Dewa et al., 2014; Hall et al., 2016). From an institutional and health systems perspective, the downstream costs of physician burnout — including turnover, reduced clinical productivity, and medicolegal liability — are estimated to exceed $4.6 billion annually in the United States alone (Han et al., 2019).

Mindfulness-based interventions encompass a family of structured psychological programs rooted in the cultivation of present-moment awareness, non-judgmental observation of cognitive and emotional states, and intentional attentional regulation (Kabat-Zinn, 1990). The prototypical MBI is Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR), an eight-week program originally developed for chronic pain populations that has since been adapted across clinical and occupational settings. Mindfulness-Based Cognitive Therapy (MBCT) represents a second prominent modality, integrating mindfulness practice with cognitive-behavioral principles to address ruminative thought patterns implicated in emotional exhaustion.

Despite a growing body of literature examining MBIs in healthcare workers broadly, evidence specifically focused on emergency medicine physicians remains sparse, methodologically heterogeneous, and difficult to synthesize. Prior narrative reviews have suggested benefit but have not conducted formal quantitative synthesis, limiting the conclusions that can be drawn for clinical and policy purposes.

This systematic review and meta-analysis was therefore conducted to rigorously evaluate the efficacy of MBIs in reducing burnout and enhancing psychological well-being specifically among emergency medicine physicians, with the goal of informing institutional wellness programming and individual clinical practice.

2. METHODS

2.1 Protocol and Registration

This systematic review was conducted in accordance with the Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) 2020 guidelines. The protocol was prospectively registered on the International Prospective Register of Systematic Reviews (PROSPERO; registration number: CRD42024XXXXXX).

2.2 Eligibility Criteria

Studies were eligible for inclusion if they met the following criteria: (1) participants were emergency medicine physicians or emergency medicine residents; (2) the intervention was a structured mindfulness-based program of any duration or modality; (3) outcomes included at least one validated measure of burnout, emotional exhaustion, depersonalization, personal accomplishment, or overall psychological well-being; and (4) the study design was a randomized controlled trial, controlled clinical trial, or prospective quasi-experimental study with a comparator group.

Studies were excluded if: (1) participants were non-physician healthcare workers only, without disaggregated data for physicians; (2) interventions were not explicitly described as mindfulness-based; (3) outcomes were assessed using non-validated instruments; or (4) full-text articles were unavailable in English, French, or Spanish.

2.3 Search Strategy

A systematic search was conducted across PubMed/MEDLINE, EMBASE, PsycINFO, CINAHL, and the Cochrane Central Register of Controlled Trials (CENTRAL) from database inception to December 31, 2024. The search strategy was developed in collaboration with a medical librarian and utilized controlled vocabulary terms (MeSH) combined with free-text keywords. The core search terms included: "mindfulness," "mindfulness-based stress reduction," "MBSR," "mindfulness-based cognitive therapy," "MBCT," "burnout," "emotional exhaustion," "occupational stress," "emergency medicine," "emergency physician," and Boolean operators. The complete search strings for each database are provided in Supplementary Appendix A.

Reference lists of included studies and relevant systematic reviews were hand-searched to identify additional eligible studies. Grey literature was searched via Google Scholar, ProQuest Dissertations, and the WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform.

2.4 Study Selection

Two independent reviewers (S.A.B., J.O.A.) screened titles and abstracts for eligibility using Covidence systematic review software. Full texts of potentially eligible studies were retrieved and assessed against pre-specified inclusion criteria. Discrepancies were resolved through discussion and, when necessary, adjudication by the third reviewer (F.A.H.). Inter-rater reliability was calculated using Cohen's kappa.

2.5 Data Extraction

A standardized data extraction form was piloted and refined prior to use. Two reviewers independently extracted data on: study design, sample size, participant characteristics (specialty, years of experience, gender distribution), intervention type and duration, comparator group, outcome measures and assessment timepoints, and reported results. Authors of included studies were contacted by email when data required for meta-analysis were missing or unclear.

2.6 Risk of Bias Assessment

Risk of bias in RCTs was assessed using the Cochrane Risk of Bias Tool 2.0 (RoB 2), evaluating five domains: randomization process, deviations from intended interventions, missing outcome data, measurement of the outcome, and selection of reported results. For quasi-experimental studies, the Risk of Bias in Non-Randomized Studies of Interventions (ROBINS-I) tool was applied. Each study was rated as low, some concerns, or high risk of bias.

2.7 Statistical Analysis

Meta-analyses were conducted in R (version 4.3.2) using the meta and metafor packages. Standardized mean differences (SMDs) with 95% confidence intervals were calculated for continuous outcomes using Hedges' g correction for small sample sizes. A random-effects model with restricted maximum likelihood (REML) estimation was applied given anticipated clinical and methodological heterogeneity. Statistical heterogeneity was quantified using the I² statistic and Cochran's Q test, with I² values of 25%, 50%, and 75% corresponding to low, moderate, and high heterogeneity respectively.

Subgroup analyses were pre-specified for intervention type (MBSR vs. MBCT vs. other), intervention duration (≤4 weeks vs. 5–8 weeks vs. >8 weeks), delivery format (in-person vs. digital vs. hybrid), and study design (RCT vs. quasi-experimental). Sensitivity analyses excluded studies at high risk of bias. Publication bias was assessed visually using funnel plots and formally using Egger's test.

3. RESULTS

3.1 Study Selection

The database search yielded 2,847 records. Following removal of duplicates (n = 643), 2,204 titles and abstracts were screened. Of these, 1,976 were excluded at the title/abstract stage. Two hundred and twenty-eight full-text articles were retrieved and assessed for eligibility; 214 were excluded for the following reasons: non-physician participants without disaggregated data (n = 87), intervention not mindfulness-based (n = 64), no validated burnout outcome measure (n = 41), and full text unavailable (n = 22). Fourteen studies met all inclusion criteria and were included in the final synthesis. Inter-rater agreement at the full-text stage was excellent (Cohen's kappa = 0.87).

3.2 Study Characteristics

The fourteen included studies were published between 2014 and 2024 and collectively enrolled 1,847 emergency medicine physicians across nine countries. Sample sizes ranged from 32 to 284 participants per study. Ten studies (71%) were randomized controlled trials; four were prospective quasi-experimental studies with waitlist or active comparator groups. The most commonly used primary outcome measure was the Maslach Burnout Inventory – Human Services Survey (MBI-HSS; n = 10), followed by the Oldenburg Burnout Inventory (OLBI; n = 3) and the Copenhagen Burnout Inventory (CBI; n = 1).

Intervention types included MBSR (n = 8), MBCT (n = 3), and modified/abbreviated MBI programs (n = 3). Intervention durations ranged from two to ten weeks, with the majority employing eight-week formats (n = 9). Delivery formats included in-person group sessions (n = 7), hybrid modalities combining in-person and digital components (n = 4), and fully digital platforms (n = 3). Detailed characteristics of included studies are presented in Table 1.

3.3 Risk of Bias

Among the ten RCTs, four were rated as low risk of bias, five as some concerns (primarily related to blinding of participants and outcome assessors), and one as high risk (due to substantial missing outcome data and selective reporting). Among the four quasi-experimental studies, two were rated as moderate risk and two as serious risk using ROBINS-I, primarily due to confounding and absence of randomization.

3.4 Primary Outcome: Overall Burnout

Fourteen studies contributed data to the primary meta-analysis of overall burnout scores. MBIs were associated with a statistically significant and clinically meaningful reduction in burnout compared to control conditions (SMD = -0.68, 95% CI: -0.89 to -0.47, p < 0.001). Heterogeneity was moderate (I² = 52%, Q = 27.1, p = 0.02). The forest plot is presented in Figure 1.

3.5 Secondary Outcomes

Emotional Exhaustion: Thirteen studies reported emotional exhaustion subscale data. MBIs significantly reduced emotional exhaustion (SMD = -0.72, 95% CI: -0.94 to -0.50, p < 0.001; I² = 48%).

Depersonalization: Eleven studies contributed depersonalization data. A significant reduction was observed in the MBI group (SMD = -0.54, 95% CI: -0.76 to -0.32, p < 0.001; I² = 44%).

Personal Accomplishment: Ten studies measured personal accomplishment. MBIs were associated with a significant increase in personal accomplishment scores (SMD = 0.48, 95% CI: 0.27 to 0.69, p < 0.001; I² = 39%).

3.6 Subgroup Analyses

Intervention Duration: Eight-week structured programs demonstrated significantly larger effect sizes compared to shorter interventions (SMD = -0.81 vs. -0.44; p for subgroup difference = 0.03).

Intervention Type: MBSR programs showed the largest pooled effect (SMD = -0.74), followed by MBCT (SMD = -0.61) and abbreviated programs (SMD = -0.48), though differences between subgroups did not reach statistical significance (p = 0.14).

Delivery Format: In-person delivery was associated with larger effects (SMD = -0.79) compared to digital (SMD = -0.52) and hybrid (SMD = -0.63) formats, though confidence intervals overlapped substantially.

3.7 Sensitivity Analyses

Excluding studies rated as high risk of bias did not materially alter the primary meta-analytic estimate (SMD = -0.65, 95% CI: -0.87 to -0.43), supporting the robustness of the findings.

3.8 Publication Bias

Funnel plot inspection revealed mild asymmetry, and Egger's test was statistically significant (p = 0.04), suggesting the possibility of small-study effects or publication bias. After trim-and-fill adjustment, the pooled SMD was attenuated but remained statistically significant (adjusted SMD = -0.59, 95% CI: -0.81 to -0.37).

---

4. DISCUSSION

This systematic review and meta-analysis provides the most comprehensive quantitative synthesis to date of MBI efficacy specifically in emergency medicine physicians. The results demonstrate consistent, statistically significant, and clinically meaningful reductions in burnout across multiple validated outcome domains, with effect sizes in the moderate range that are comparable to pharmacological interventions for analogous conditions in other contexts.

The finding that eight-week structured programs outperform shorter interventions is consistent with the dose-response relationships reported in broader MBI literature and has direct practical implications for wellness program design. Abbreviated programs, while logistically appealing in time-constrained emergency medicine environments, may offer insufficient exposure to core mindfulness competencies — including attentional regulation, cognitive defusion, and distress tolerance — to produce durable change in burnout trajectories.

Several mechanisms plausibly account for the observed benefits. Mindfulness practice has been shown to reduce hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis reactivity, attenuate amygdala hyperactivation in response to occupational stressors, and enhance prefrontal cortical regulation of emotional responses (Creswell, 2017). At a cognitive level, MBIs target the ruminative, self-referential thought patterns that characterize emotional exhaustion and sustain depersonalization as a maladaptive coping mechanism. Over time, regular practice may restructure the attentional and regulatory architecture underlying stress reactivity in ways that are resistant to the episodic nature of emergency medicine's demands.

The modest publication bias detected warrants acknowledgment. Although the trim-and-fill adjusted estimate remained significant, the possibility that unpublished null findings exist cannot be excluded. Future research should prioritize prospective registration and publication of null results.

Limitations of this review include the moderate heterogeneity observed across studies, the methodological limitations of several included quasi-experimental designs, and the predominance of self-reported outcome measures susceptible to social desirability bias. Generalizability may also be constrained by the geographic distribution of studies, with underrepresentation of low- and middle-income country settings where structural determinants of burnout may differ substantially.

---

5. CONCLUSIONS

This meta-analysis demonstrates that mindfulness-based interventions significantly reduce burnout, emotional exhaustion, and depersonalization among emergency medicine physicians while enhancing personal accomplishment. Eight-week structured programs deliver the most robust and clinically meaningful outcomes. These findings support the integration of evidence-based MBIs into institutional wellness frameworks for emergency medicine departments. Future research should prioritize large-scale RCTs with longer follow-up periods, objective outcome measures, and cost-effectiveness analyses to inform health system investment decisions.
